Sangam Governance · Water 1st–4th c. CE

Power is Downstream of the Land

Avvaiyar — on the ridge of a field · c. 100 BCE–300 CE
வரப்பு உயர, நீர் உயரும்;
நீர் உயர, நெல் உயரும்;
நெல் உயர, குடி உயரும்;
குடி உயர, கோன் உயர்வான்.
Varappu uyara, nīr uyarum;
Nīr uyara, nel uyarum;
Nel uyara, kuṭi uyarum;
Kuṭi uyara, kōṉ uyarvāṉ.
As the ridge of the field rises, its capacity to hold water rises.
As water capacity rises, crops grow well.
As crops grow well, people live better.
As people live better, the kingdom prospers.
As the kingdom prospers, the king himself rises.
Avvaiyar (poetess, Sangam era) — on agricultural governance · c. 100 BCE–300 CE
Governance as ecology: The king is the last consequence of the system, not its origin. Power sits downstream of water, which sits downstream of soil, which depends on the decision to build and maintain a field-bund. Infrastructure and ethics are the same thing here — one ridge holds everything.
Thiruvalluvar, Tirukkural — Van Sirappu (In Praise of Rain), couplet 1 · c. 1st–5th century CE
நீர் இன்று அமையாது உலகு என்னும் — வான் சிறப்பு
ஊர் இன்று அமையா உயிர்.
Nīr iṉṟu amaiyātu ulaku eṉṉum — vāṉ ciṟappu
Ūr iṉṟu amaiyā uyir.
There is no world without water — this is the greatness of rain.
As a village cannot exist without its people,
life cannot exist without rain.
Thiruvalluvar — Tirukkural, chapter on rain (Van Sirappu) · c. 1st–5th century CE
Thiruvalluvar devotes an entire chapter of the Tirukkural to rain as the condition for everything else. Charity and penance will both cease, he writes, if the clouds are not charitable. Climate and ethics are linked here. The collapse of one is the collapse of the other.

Senai Thottu Vaithal Tamil Birth · Marriage

You are a person of a particular water

Senai Thottu Vaithal — oral practice, Tamil Nadu · origins uncertain, attested from early Sangam period
[வாய்மொழி வழக்கு — எழுத்து வடிவமற்றது]
Caṇai Toṭṭu Vaittal — oral practice, no fixed written source
At birth, the first thing placed on the newborn's tongue is water from the village's own source, its well, its tank, its river.
Tamil oral tradition · origins pre-Sangam, documentation ongoing
A person is introduced to their community through its water. The tank or well a child first tastes is the place they come from.
Region
Tamil Nadu — widespread practice
Thread
Place-based identity · Watershed as community

Forest Baiga Central India

Asking the forest before the harvest

Among certain Baiga communities in Madhya Pradesh, the first cut of beedi leaf is preceded by a spoken address to the tree — a request, not a command. The act carries an obligation: if the forest gives, something must return. This is not ceremony for its own sake. It is a protocol for staying in relationship.

Region
Madhya Pradesh · oral tradition, undated
Thread
Consent and reciprocity
