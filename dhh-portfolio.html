Research Overview

DHH children in India face significant barriers to accessing quality education due to a lack of tailored teaching methods, resources, and support systems. This project aimed to understand these barriers and identify opportunities for design intervention — focusing on how this problem affects DHH children, their parents, and their teachers.

Research Goals
→Understand current teaching methodologies for DHH children in India
→Understand learning opportunities beyond school
→Evaluate accessibility and effectiveness of existing tools and apps for DHH children
Interviews Interviewed teachers from primary, secondary, and senior secondary levels — asking about subjects taught, teaching methods, and assessment approaches for HI students
Observation Observed students in Class II classes and visited the school on an activity day; noted classroom structures, group dynamics, and teacher–student interaction
Curriculum Study Studied the NCERT curriculum to understand expected learning outcomes and identify gaps for HI students; also looked at resources for adapting outcomes
Expert Consultation Consulted with an Audiologist and SLP, and observed parents at the clinic to gain expert opinions and understand challenges faced by families
Literature Review Reviewed research papers on educating deaf/HoH students in India, psychological characteristics of HI children, and case studies on using assistive devices
UDL Research Researched Universal Design for Learning principles to inform our design approach for creating flexible, inclusive learning environments

Challenges in Teaching & Learning

Student Learning Difficulties
Pacing & New Content Missed questions; difficulty with volume of new English words Need for pauses and repetition due to lower language exposure
Abstract Concepts Difficulty with new shapes in Maths Subjects with logical/abstract concepts require extra attention for HI students
Lack of Visuals Huge amounts of text, lack of supporting visuals Importance of visual aids
Varied Abilities Varying degrees of HI; some with additional mental disabilities Need for individual attention and assessment
Parental Challenges
Online Resources Much online learning content for support was outdated Need for updated, accessible content
Tuition Access Difficulty finding suitable tuitions for DHH children Need for individualised environment for child
Assessment Challenges
Written Assessments Even school assessments are primarily written, with limited recitation Need for multiple channels of assessment so children can express themselves

The Intervention: A Smartphone-Enabled App

To address the multifaceted issues identified, our primary proposed intervention is a smartphone-enabled application — a widely accessible solution that removes overhead costs of physical materials and leverages technology for greater reach. Printable learning materials using gamified principles are also in development for students without stable smartphone access.

Reflections & Learnings

Key Takeaway

"Visuals and ISL are not supplementary tools — they are the primary language of learning in this context."

We learned the critical importance of visuals and ISL as fundamental tools for learning and communication in this context. We understood the diverse needs within the HI community, acknowledging varying levels of hearing impairment and co-occurring disabilities.

The research highlighted the existing strengths in teaching — such as the use of visuals and practical skills — while also revealing areas where support is lacking, particularly for parents who are largely excluded from the educational ecosystem.

The decision to pursue a smartphone-enabled application was a direct outcome of considering constraints like cost-effectiveness and accessibility, while leveraging technology to overcome limitations. Ultimately, the project honed our ability to conduct comprehensive user research, synthesise complex findings, and translate them into a justified, user-centred design solution aimed at addressing a significant educational challenge.
